Hansel and Gretel

an Interactive Children's Book Experience

Project Walkthrough

Purpose

As part of my Digital Narrative and Interactive Design class, our team embarked on bringing a scene from a children's storybook to life. Collaborating with two other team members, we collectively planned, constructed, and implemented the project. The central goal was to identify that crucial 'page-turn' moment in the story, a pivotal element in narrative structure. For our project, we chose the scene from Hansel and Gretel where the witch tosses the children into the oven. Our task was to animate this scene and incorporate two interactions allowing users to influence the outcome of the narrative. Our primary focus was on creating a user experience that would be accessible and comprehensible to anyone interacting with the exhibit. With these considerations in mind, we started the construction process.

Medium

The main interactive component was coded using the Arduino UNO, and I was responsible for both the code development and assembling the accompanying hardware. The process involved iterative assembly and disassembly of components to determine the most efficient implementation. The primary hardware components used in the project included two servo motors, two LED lights, a push button, and a hall sensor. Both the hall sensor and push button played integral roles in the interactivity of the design.

Constructing the physical layout of the hardware presented challenges, particularly concerning the need for components to function beyond the confines of the given breadboard. The size of our house exceeded the surface area of the board, requiring creative solutions to extend the functionality of the components. Additionally, positioning the push button posed a unique challenge as it was crucial to the interactive process. We aimed for visibility and standalone functionality for user understanding, leading to a struggle in finding a suitable placement that made sense for users while still being connected to the breadboard. This process involved careful maneuvering and compromise, especially considering that the available wires were not long enough for our initial placement plan.

For the LEDs, our goal was to make them removable from the breadboard and flexible enough to be positioned inside the oven. The plan involved incorporating three LEDs—two red and one yellow—arranged in a row and situated on the floorboard away from the breadboard. To achieve this setup, we needed to create a wire that would split into three branches. This would result in a linear circuit, and despite the potential risk due to the limited length of the LEDs, we deemed it worthwhile for the desired lighting effect.

Learning Curves

One of the significant learning challenges we faced was troubleshooting the physical components alongside the working code. There were instances where the program was syntactically correct, but the hardware malfunctioned in some way, leading to confusion. Additionally, comprehending the actual implementation of the hardware differed from merely having the parts function independently. As we integrated the hardware into our designed space, we had to make adjustments to ensure that it continued to function as intended.
